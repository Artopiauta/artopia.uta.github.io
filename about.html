Art Therapy

For our nation’s veterans who are receiving psychiatric care for PTSD and other emotional conditions, art therapy can be an effective form of treatment, either as an adjunct to other therapies or as a form of individual or group psychotherapy (arttherapy.org). In 2015, the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs listed California, Florida, and Texas as having the highest populations of veterans in the U.S. (Quick facts, 2017). However, according to the American Art Therapy Association there are no undergraduate or graduate art therapy degree programs approved by the Art Therapy Educational Program Approval Board (EPAB) at Texas universities (arttherapy.org). In the Dallas-Ft. Worth (DFW) metroplex there are limited off-campus organizations specializing in art therapy. The Art Station in Ft. Worth is the most established, well known. The lack of art therapy degree programs, certificates, and on/off-campus programs in DFW and at Texas universities provided an opportunity for the development of an art therapy program at the University of Texas at Arlington (UTA). With a sustainable veteran workshop program, eventually students in the Art and Art History Department focusing on art therapy will be able to learn from, observe and interact to gain invaluable knowledge for their own studies.

The newly conceived UTA Art Therapy program, which includes creating and establishing art therapy workshops with licensed art therapists from The Art Station, for the 4,900 veterans enrolled is also a way for researchers to collect data to determine the magnitude of symptom relief and healing for the men and women veterans dealing with service-related trauma or disabilities. Researchers are conducting a Profile of Mood States (POMS) pre/post test survey (Shacham, 1983; Curran, Andrykowski & Studts, 1995) with veterans before and after their participation in the workshops. Additionally, the researchers are interviewing and collecting testimonials and photos for data, videos, and this website.

“The impact of a program at UTA, which enrolls the most veterans in the state, is beginning to provide a snapshot of its importance,” said Dr. Amanda Alexander, Principal Investigator. “After several art therapy workshops, we have had a very positive response from those participating.” One such participant is veteran Sherry Knutson, who remarked, “The UTA art therapy workshops provide me the opportunity to focus my thoughts on creating a meaningful art project, which in turn helps to quiet the PTSD symptom of hyper-awareness and allows me to relax and live in the moment.”
